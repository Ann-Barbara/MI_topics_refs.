Article 1 – Smith et al. (2021)

Introduction: Examines neurobiological mechanisms of aerobic exercise on depression  
Methods:30 participants; 8-week moderate aerobic program; measured BDNF and depressive symptoms  
Results: Increased BDNF; decreased depressive symptoms  
Discussion:Suggests neurogenesis may mediate mood improvement  

Summary: 
This study explores how aerobic exercise impacts depression through biological mechanisms. Over an 8-week program, participants showed increased levels of Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF), alongside reduced depressive symptoms. The findings suggest that exercise may improve mental health by promoting neurogenesis. However, other factors such as therapy or medication may have influenced the results.

Cross-exam mini-checklist:
- Falsifier: No change in depressive symptoms despite verified exercise compliance  
- Strongest confound: Concurrent psychotherapy or medication  


 Article 2 – Brown et al. (2019)

Introduction: Social engagement in sports and mental health outcomes  
Methods:University students; group vs individual exercise; surveys over 12 weeks  
Results:Group participants reported lower depressive symptoms  
Discussion: Social support mediates positive outcomes  

Summary:
This study investigates the role of social interaction in exercise and its effect on depression among university students. Results showed that students participating in group exercise reported lower depressive symptoms compared to those exercising alone. This suggests that social support enhances the mental health benefits of physical activity. However, pre-existing social networks may have influenced the findings.

Cross-exam mini-checklist:
- Falsifier: No difference between group and solo exercisers  
- Strongest confound: Pre-existing social network differences
